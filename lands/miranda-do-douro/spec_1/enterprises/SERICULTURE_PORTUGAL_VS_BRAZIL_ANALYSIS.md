# Sericulture: Portugal vs. Brazil - Why the Difference?

## Why Sericulture Works in Brazil But Not (As Well) in Portugal

You're absolutely right to question this! Sericulture IS profitable in Brazil. Here's why the economics are completely different:

---

## BRAZIL SERICULTURE SUCCESS FACTORS

### 1. **Established Industry Infrastructure** ✓✓✓

**Brazil**:
- ✓ Large-scale silk industry (especially Paraná state)
- ✓ Cooperatives that buy cocoons at guaranteed prices
- ✓ Processing facilities nearby (farmers sell raw cocoons, don't process)
- ✓ Technical support from EMBRAPA and cooperatives
- ✓ Established supply chains (eggs, equipment, feed supplements)
- ✓ **Farmers sell fresh cocoons immediately** → No processing, no storage

**Portugal**:
- ❌ NO commercial silk industry
- ❌ NO cooperatives buying cocoons
- ❌ NO processing facilities
- ❌ NO technical support infrastructure
- ❌ You must find buyers yourself or process yourself (expensive equipment)
- ❌ **You're building from zero**

**Impact**: In Brazil, you harvest cocoons and sell them the next day. In Portugal, you must either:
- Process into silk yourself (€10,000-20,000 equipment + skills)
- Find artisan buyers (limited, unstable market)
- Create value-added products yourself (scarves, crafts - high skill)

---

### 2. **Climate Advantage** ✓✓

**Brazil (especially Paraná, São Paulo)**:
- ✓ Consistent warm springs (20-25°C) - ideal for silkworms
- ✓ Long growing season for mulberry (year-round in some regions)
- ✓ Predictable temperatures (less risk of cold snaps killing worms)
- ✓ High humidity during rearing season (worms need 70-80% humidity)
- ✓ Can do **3-5 cycles per year** in some regions

**Portugal (Miranda do Douro)**:
- ⚠ Variable spring temperatures (10-25°C, frosts possible April-May)
- ⚠ Hot dry summers (low humidity, stress on mulberries)
- ⚠ Cold winters (limits season)
- ⚠ Can do **1-2 cycles per year maximum**
- ⚠ Higher risk of crop failure due to temperature swings

**Impact**: Brazilian farms can produce 3-5 cocoon harvests per year. Portuguese farms: 1-2 maximum. **3× revenue difference from climate alone.**

---

### 3. **Labor Cost Difference** ✓

**Brazil**:
- ✓ Lower labor costs
- ✓ Often family labor on small farms (already farming other crops)
- ✓ Sericulture integrated with other activities (coffee, cattle, etc.)
- ✓ Labor-intensive activities economically viable

**Portugal**:
- ⚠ Higher labor costs (EU wage expectations)
- ⚠ Opportunity cost: Your time could earn €8-15/hour in other work
- ⚠ Labor-intensive enterprises need higher returns to justify

**Impact**: If sericulture pays €5/hour labor in Brazil (acceptable), same €5/hour in Portugal is below minimum wage (€4.75/hour in 2026, but realistically need €8-10/hour to be viable).

---

### 4. **Market Size and Demand** ✓✓✓

**Brazil**:
- ✓ Domestic textile industry buying silk
- ✓ Export market (China, India)
- ✓ Guaranteed prices from cooperatives (price stability)
- ✓ Large enough market to absorb production

**Portugal**:
- ❌ No domestic silk textile industry
- ❌ Very small artisan/craft market
- ❌ No price guarantees
- ❌ Must create your own market (difficult and uncertain)

**Impact**: Brazilian farmers have buyers lined up. Portuguese farmers must find buyers, negotiate prices, or become artisans themselves.

---

### 5. **Scale Economics** ✓

**Brazil** (typical sericulture farm):
- 1,000-3,000 mulberry trees
- 100,000-300,000 silkworms per cycle
- 3-5 cycles per year
- **Production**: 500-2,000 kg cocoons/year
- **Revenue**: R$20,000-80,000/year (€3,500-14,000)
- **Profit margin**: 30-40% after costs
- **Net profit**: R$6,000-30,000/year (€1,000-5,000)

**Portugal** (realistic small-scale):
- 50-100 mulberry trees (starting)
- 10,000-20,000 silkworms per cycle
- 1-2 cycles per year maximum
- **Production**: 15-40 kg cocoons/year
- **Revenue**: €300-1,200/year (IF you find buyers at Brazilian prices)
- **Profit margin**: 20-30% (higher costs, no infrastructure support)
- **Net profit**: €60-360/year

**Impact**: Scale matters. Brazil operations are 10-50× larger, with infrastructure support.

---

### 6. **Integration with Existing Agriculture** ✓

**Brazil**:
- ✓ Sericulture often added to existing farms (coffee, cattle, poultry)
- ✓ Mulberry leaves used as cattle/goat fodder (dual purpose)
- ✓ Silkworm waste (pupae) sold as animal feed (additional income)
- ✓ Cocoon processing waste used in compost
- ✓ **Diversification of existing operation**, not standalone

**Portugal**:
- You'd be starting from scratch
- Harder to integrate without existing farming infrastructure
- Less developed market for byproducts

---

**Reasons**:

- Small market

### ⚠️ MAYBE - As artisan/craft/niche business

**Conditions for success**:

2. **You build premium market** (€50-200+ for silk scarves, luxury craft items)

5. **You start very small** (20-30 trees trial) while focusing on other enterprises

**Revenue potential** (artisan route):
- €3,000-8,000/year IF you become skilled artisan with established market
- 3-5 years to build skills and market
- High risk, requires artistic talent + marketing skills

### ✓✓ YES - Plant mulberries for other purposes, silk as byproduct

**Best strategy for Portugal**:
1. **Plant 50-100 mulberry trees** for fruit and fodder (primary value)
2. **Trial 1,000-2,000 silkworms** in Year 3-4 (experiment, learning)
3. **IF you love it AND find market** → Scale slowly to artisan production
4. **IF not** → Still have valuable fruit and fodder trees
