## PART 2: COLD ROOM / ROOT CELLAR SYSTEM

### Michelle & Cody's Setup (CRITICAL LEARNING)

**What They Built**:
- Walled off corner of basement (two walls = earth contact, two walls = insulated framed walls)
- Size: Approximately 8-10m² (TINY!)
- Cost: ~$500-1,000 USD (€460-920) - DIY lumber, insulation
- Temperature: Maintains 50-55°F (10-13°C) year-round (passive, no electricity)
- Humidity: High (60-90%) - essential for root vegetables

**Critical Features**:
1. **Two walls touching earth** (stable temperature from ground - 50°F = 10°C year-round)
2. **Two walls super-insulated** (prevents basement heat from entering)
3. **VENTILATION** (absolutely critical - rotting vegetables emit gases that can kill you!)
   - Two vents: One low (cool air in), one high (warm air out)
   - Creates passive air circulation

**What They Store**:
- 90 kg potatoes (in wooden crates, single layer during curing)
- 30-40 kg onions (hanging in mesh bags OR on shelves)
- 50-100 kg squash (on shelves, single layer)
- 55 kg carrots (in separate chest freezer set to 35-40°F, in plastic bags - NOT frozen!)

**Separate Rooms**:
- Potatoes/squash/carrots in one room
- Onions in separate area (onions emit gases that make potatoes sprout faster!)

## PART 3: CURING TECHNIQUES (CRITICAL for Storage Success)

### Michelle's Emphasis: "Curing is THE SECRET"

**What is Curing?**
- Allowing harvested vegetables to sit in warm, dry, ventilated area (2-4 weeks)
- **Purpose**:
  - Thickens skin (prevents moisture loss, rot)
  - Heals cuts/bruises
  - Allows excess moisture to evaporate
  - Triggers dormancy (vegetables "go to sleep" = store longer)

---

### Curing Methods by Crop

#### **Potatoes** (White/Yellow Varieties)

**Michelle's Method**:
1. Harvest when plants die back (foliage yellowed, dry)
2. Brush off dirt (DON'T wash!)
3. Spread in single layer on shelves in cold room (or porch if weather warm/dry)
4. Temperature: 50-60°F (10-15°C)
5. Humidity: 80-90% (high)
6. Duration: **2 weeks**
7. After curing: Move to crates/boxes in cold room (45-50°F = 7-10°C)

**Results**: 6-10 month storage (September harvest → June/July)

**Application to Miranda do Douro**:
- Your climate: Hot September (25-30°C) = cure on shaded porch (north side building)
- Move to cold room after 2 weeks
- Expected storage: 6-8 months (slightly shorter due to warmer fall, but still excellent)

---

#### **Sweet Potatoes**

**Michelle's Method**:
1. Harvest before first frost (CRITICAL - frost kills sweet potatoes!)
2. Handle gently (skin very delicate)
3. Cure on porch in **WARM, SUNNY location** (sweet potatoes need heat to cure!)
4. Temperature: 80-85°F (27-29°C) - WARMER than white potatoes
5. Humidity: 80-90%
6. Duration: **2 weeks**
7. After curing: Store in cold room (55-60°F = 13-15°C) - WARMER than white potatoes

**Results**: 6-10 month storage

**Application to Miranda do Douro**:
- Your climate: Perfect for sweet potato curing (September still warm = 25-30°C)
- Cure on south-facing porch (full sun)
- Store in slightly warmer area of cold room (or separate box insulated)

---

#### **Onions**

**Michelle's Method**:
1. Harvest when tops fall over, dry down
2. Pull from ground, shake off dirt
3. Lay in single layer in well-ventilated area (full sun OK)
4. Duration: **2-4 weeks** until outer skin papery, dry, tops completely dry
5. Cut tops off (leave 2-3cm stem)
6. Store in mesh bags OR crates in cold room (separate from potatoes!)

**Results**: 8-12 month storage

**Application to Miranda do Douro**:
- Your September climate: Hot, dry (PERFECT for onion curing)
- Lay on porch, full sun OK (onions love sun during curing)
- Expected storage: 8-12 months (your dry continental climate = excellent for onions)

---

#### **Butternut Squash**

**Michelle's Method**:
1. Harvest when skin hard (fingernail can't puncture), stem dry/corky
2. Leave 5-8cm stem attached (DON'T pull off - creates entry for rot)
3. Cure on porch in **warm, sunny location**
4. Temperature: 70-80°F (21-27°C)
5. Duration: **2 weeks**
6. After curing: Store in cold room (50-55°F = 10-13°C)

**Results**: 6-8 months storage (October → May/June)

**Quote from Michelle**: "Way better than pumpkins. Butternut squash you can leave in the field for weeks and they self-cure. Pumpkins rot in 2-3 weeks."

**Application to Miranda do Douro**:
- Your October climate: Still warm (15-20°C) = good for curing
- Squash tolerates light frost (unlike sweet potatoes)
- Expected storage: 6-8 months

---

#### **Carrots** (SPECIAL METHOD)

**Michelle's Method** (BRILLIANT - 10 month storage!):
1. Harvest carrots
2. **DON'T wash** (dirt is protective layer)
3. Put directly into plastic bags (thick freezer bags)
4. **TAPE UP all air holes** (creates humid microclimate)
5. Store in chest freezer set to 35-40°F (2-4°C) - NOT frozen, just very cold

**Why This Works**:
- Carrots need COLD (35-40°F) + HIGH humidity (95%+) to store
- Plastic bag traps moisture (you want condensation inside bag!)
- Chest freezer = stable cold temp (regular fridge too dry)

**Results**: **10 MONTHS storage** (August harvest → June next year)

**Application to Miranda do Douro**:

**Option 1**: Chest freezer method (copy Michelle exactly)
- Investment: €200-400 (used chest freezer)
- Set to warmest setting (just above freezing)
- Cost: €50-80/year electricity

**Option 2**: Cold room + high humidity (if root cellar very humid)
- Store carrots in plastic bags in coldest part of root cellar
- Expected storage: 4-6 months (less than freezer method, but still good)

**Option 3**: Sand storage (traditional method)
- Layer carrots in boxes with damp sand between layers
- Store in cold room
- Expected storage: 4-6 months

**Recommendation**: Start with Option 2 or 3 (use root cellar), add chest freezer Year 2 if want extended carrot storage (October → August)

## PART 4: BATCH PRODUCTION PHILOSOPHY (Time Management)

### Michelle's Key Strategy: "I Don't Do Succession Plantings"

**Standard Gardening Advice**: Plant small amounts every 2 weeks for continuous harvest
- Example: 3m row of lettuce every 2 weeks = fresh lettuce all season

**Michelle's Approach**: Plant BIG once, harvest ALL at once, preserve
- Example: 30m² sweet corn planted once = 2 massive preserving days, done for year

---

### Why Batch Production Works for Busy Families

**Michelle's Reasoning**:
1. **Time Efficiency**:
   - 2 big preserving days (8-10 hours each) = 16-20 hours total
   - vs Continuous harvest + daily cooking = 5-10 hours/week × 12 weeks = 60-120 hours
   - **Saves 40-100 hours per crop!**

2. **Mental Load**:
   - One big effort → done → don't think about it again for a year
   - vs Constant "I need to pick beans again" every 3 days for 3 months

3. **Meal Simplicity**:
   - Winter meals EASIER than summer (pull preserved food from freezer/cold room, cook)
   - Summer meals = "What's ready? What needs picking? What needs using before it goes bad?"

4. **Focus**:
   - Separate gardening season from homeschooling (May-October = garden, October-May = homeschool)
   - Doesn't split attention between two demanding tasks

---

### Crops Michelle Does in BIG Batches

| Crop | Planting | Harvest Window | Preservation | Storage |
|------|----------|----------------|--------------|---------|
| **Sweet Corn** | One 30m² block, May | 2-week window, July | 2 days blanching/freezing | 12 months frozen |
| **Green Beans** | 20m² every other year | 3-week window | 2-3 days blanching/freezing | 12 months frozen |
| **Broccoli** | 30 plants, May | 2-3 week window | 1 day blanching/freezing | 12 months frozen |
| **Raspberries** | Fall-bearing only | 3-4 week window, Sept | 2-3 days freezing/jam | 12 months frozen/canned |
| **Tomatoes** | 15 plants | 6-8 week window, Aug-Sept | 4-6 days canning (spread over weeks) | 12 months canned |

**Key Insight**: Michelle chooses crops that:
- ✓ Can harvest in bulk (100+ lbs at once)
- ✓ Preserve easily (blanch + freeze OR can OR dry)
- ✓ Store long-term (12 months)

- Compacted

4. **Cover Crops** (winter, in unused beds)
   - Radishes ("My family doesn't like radishes, so it's a cover crop")

## PART 7: DRIED BEANS (Ultimate Self-Sufficiency Crop)

### Michelle's Emphasis: "This Is THE Self-Sufficiency Food"

**Why Dried Beans Are Perfect**:
1. ✅ **High protein** (15-25% protein by weight = meat alternative)
2. ✅ **Filling** (fiber, complex carbs = satiety)
3. ✅ **ZERO preservation needed** (let dry on plant, shell, store in bucket)
4. ✅ **Stores for YEARS** (5-10 years in sealed buckets)
5. ✅ **Easy to grow** (like green beans, but let mature/dry)
6. ✅ **Nitrogen-fixing** (improves soil, no fertilizer needed)
7. ✅ **Versatile** (soups, stews, refried beans, salads, etc.)

**Michelle's Varieties**:
- Navy beans (small, white, for baked beans/soups)
- Kidney beans (red, large, for chili)
- Tiger's Eye beans (yellow/brown striped, beautiful, mild flavor)

---

### Michelle's Harvest/Processing Method (GENIUS)

**Traditional Method** (what most people do):
- Pick each pod individually
- Shell beans by hand (squeeze pod, pop out beans)
- **Time**: 10-20 hours for 20 kg beans (extremely tedious)

**Michelle's Method**:
1. Let beans dry completely on plant (pods brown, crispy, beans rattle inside)
2. Pull entire plants, throw in pillowcases (old fabric bags)
3. Have kids **whack pillowcases against porch** (beans fall out of pods!)
4. Open pillowcase: Beans at bottom, empty pods on top
5. Dump pods, keep beans
6. Spread beans on window screens for 1 week (ensure 100% dry)
7. Store in food-grade buckets

**Time**: 2-3 hours for 20 kg beans (vs 10-20 hours traditional method!)

**Quote**: "This takes minutes. The hours I could have saved." (Interviewer's reaction)

## PART 8: SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES TO ADOPT

### 1. **Square-Plot Corn Planting** (vs Rows)

**Michelle's Method**: Plant corn in square/block (e.g., 5m × 6m = 30m²)
**Why**: Corn is wind-pollinated, pollen blows sideways → square = better pollination than long rows
**Result**: Fuller ears, better kernel development

**Application**: If growing corn (not in Year 1 plan, but possible Year 2+)
- Plant in block, not rows
- Improves yield 10-20%

### 2. **Tomato Blight Prevention** (Critical for Humid Climates)

**Michelle's Challenges**: Ohio = humid summers, blight is MAJOR problem
**Her Solutions**:
1. **Vertical trellising** (fence/cattle panel, 2m tall)
   - Keeps all foliage OFF ground
   - Airflow reduces humidity around plant
2. **Prune bottom leaves** (stems bare up to 30cm from ground)
   - Soil-borne blight spores don't splash onto leaves in rain
3. **Mulch soil heavily** (10 cm leaves/straw)
   - Further prevents splash-up
4. **Weekly spray**: Organic fungicide + liquid seaweed + drop of dish soap
   - Preventative, not reactive

**Result**: Healthy tomatoes all season (July-October)

**Application to Miranda do Douro**:
- Your climate: Drier than Ohio (400-500mm rain vs 1,000mm) = LESS blight pressure
- Still use techniques: Vertical trellising, mulch, prune bottom leaves
- Spray: Probably only needed if wet year

### 3. **Cold Frame for Extended Greens** (Brilliant Low-Cost Setup)

**Michelle's Design**:
- Location: South-facing wall of house (reflects heat + sun)
- Structure: Cinder blocks (3-4 layers high = 40-60cm)
- Top: Salvaged windows (laid across cinder blocks)
- Planting: Greens (lettuce, spinach, kale, cilantro) planted September

**How It Works**:
- Sun hits south wall → heats air in cold frame
- Windows trap heat (greenhouse effect)
- Thermal mass (cinder blocks + house foundation) releases heat at night
- Keeps greens from freezing (down to -5°C outside, stays 5-10°C inside)

**Result**: Fresh greens until December (vs typical freeze-out in October)

**Cost**: €50-150 (cinder blocks €1-2 each, windows free/salvaged)

**Application to YOUR Farm**:
- Perfect for Miranda winters (cold but not extreme)
- Extends cilantro season (for Brazilian market - high demand in winter!)
- Location: South wall of your 1980s building
- **Revenue**: Extra 2-3 months cilantro sales = €500-1,200

### 3. **Cross-Pollination of Corn**

**Michelle's Challenge**:
- Grows sweet corn (for eating fresh)
- Grows dent corn (for cornmeal/flour)

- All corn cross-pollinates if flowering at same time = ruins seed/flavor
