1. The most profitable “proven” activity categories in Portugal

There is no single public ranking of “most profitable” because profitability depends heavily on water, region, scale, labor, subsidies, processing, direct sales, and debt. Portugal’s official farm-accounting source for real farm economics is RICA, which collects harmonized farm income and cost data; for market prices, SIMA publishes weekly agricultural price information.

Still, the strongest proven commercial categories to investigate are:

Activity	Why it is commercially strong	Main limitation
Protected horticulture: tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, salads, herbs	High output per hectare; fast cash flow; can supply restaurants, markets, boxes, shops	Needs irrigation, plastic/greenhouse legality, labor, sales discipline
Fruit + berries: blueberries, raspberries, strawberries, figs, citrus, cherries	Fruits/vegetables are among Portugal’s major agricultural sectors; high-value exports favor fruit and processed goods	Labor, irrigation, frost/heat risk, harvest logistics
Olive oil	Portugal is strong in olive oil; premium oil can be branded and value-added	Needs suitable climate, harvesting equipment/co-op access, years to maturity
Wine / grapes	One of Portugal’s largest production sectors; high value when combined with DOC, tourism, tastings, direct sales	Licensing, capital, branding, long payback, competition
Tree nuts: almond, walnut, pistachio, hazelnut	Portugal has active nut production and export/reprocessing activity; almonds, pistachios, and walnuts are commercially relevant	Water during establishment, frost, mechanization, years to production
Cork oak / montado	Portugal is the world’s largest cork producer, about half of global production; cork has strong export demand and many uses	Very slow: first harvest around 25 years, then roughly 9-year bark cycles
Pork, poultry, eggs	Pork and poultry are among Portugal’s larger production sectors	Feed dependency, sanitary rules, animal housing, slaughter/processing rules
Agro-tourism / rural tourism / horse experiences	Tourism remains a major Portuguese economic pillar; 2025 had 32.5 million guests and €29.1bn in receipts	Requires licensing, access, guest facilities, insurance, marketing
Nursery plants / seedlings / grafted trees / herbs	High value per m², modular, fits small land, supports your own farm establishment	Market development and technical skill
Value-added processing: preserves, dried fruit, olive oil, vinegar, honey, cheese, smoked products	Processing captures more margin than raw commodity sales; agri-food exports increasingly reward differentiated high-value products	Food safety, licensing, kitchen/processing rules

My first ranking for a family-scale farm would be: 1) protected horticulture + herbs, 2) nursery plants, 3) eggs/poultry/rabbits where legal, 4) mushrooms, 5) berries/small fruit if water exists, 6) olive oil or nuts as medium-term assets, 7) agro-tourism/horse experiences if the parcel has beauty/access, 8) cork/chestnut/walnut/forest systems as long-term capital.

2. Cork tree, cinnamon, horse tours — quick assessment

Cork oak is a strong long-term enterprise, especially when combined with silvopasture, sheep, goats, pigs, honey, mushrooms, acorns, biodiversity tourism, and fire-resilient agroforestry. It is not a fast-return crop. Treat cork as a 25+ year land-capital strategy, not the main early cash flow.

Cinnamon is interesting but probably not suitable for most mainland Portugal, especially not colder interior areas. Cinnamon wants tropical conditions: 25–35°C, rainfall above about 1,875 mm or strong irrigation, and 75–85% humidity. In Portugal, I would only test it as a greenhouse/conservatory crop, or perhaps in frost-free Madeira/Azores-type conditions, not as an open-field commercial crop in the north/interior.

Horse tourism is much more realistic. Best forms: guided trail rides, riding lessons, equine therapy/wellness retreats, multi-day rural routes, border trails near Spain, donkey/horse trekking, and farm-stay packages. This is strongest if the land has scenery, safe trails, accommodation potential, road access, and nearby tourism demand. It is also legally heavier than it looks: insurance, animal welfare, tourist activity registration, riding safety, and possibly rural tourism licensing.

5. For Miranda do Douro / Terra Fria Transmontana specifically

Because your initial project region is Miranda do Douro, the shortlist should be different from coastal Algarve or irrigated Alentejo. Your project guidelines already flag the region’s altitude, winter cold, summer drought, Douro Internacional constraints, water sources, access, and Spanish-border market routes as core decision factors. Terra Fria Transmontana includes Bragança, Miranda do Douro, Mogadouro, Vimioso, and Vinhais, and is recognized as a distinct agroecological/cultural landscape.

Better fits for Miranda/interior northeast:

Chestnut, walnut, almond in suitable microclimates.
Olive only on warmer slopes/lower frost-risk sites.
Grapes/wine on appropriate slopes and exposures.
Aromatic/medicinal plants: lavender, rosemary, thyme, sage, oregano.
Sheep/goats with rotational grazing.
Horses/donkeys for tourism and trekking.
Bees, especially with diverse flowering systems.
Mushrooms using indoor rooms or shaded log systems.
Eggs/rabbits/seasonal vegetables for fast learning.
Nursery plants, especially hardy fruit/nut trees.
Agro-tourism linked to Douro Internacional, Mirandese culture, border routes, dark skies, traditional food.

Higher-risk in Miranda/interior northeast:

Cinnamon, avocado, banana, tropicals.
Berries without reliable summer water and frost protection.
Intensive horticulture without legal water and greenhouse permission.
Dairy/cheese unless you are ready for daily labor and licensing.
Large-scale cork unless the parcel is genuinely suitable and you accept very long payback.
6. My recommended enterprise stack

For a resilient family-scale farm, I would not choose one activity. I would design layers:

Fast cash / learning layer, 0–12 months: eggs, seasonal vegetables, herbs, microgreens, mushrooms, seedlings, honey trial, workshops.

Medium-term layer, 1–4 years: goats/sheep, orchard establishment, berries only if water is strong, nursery expansion, value-added preserves/dried herbs, horse tourism trial.

Long-term asset layer, 4–25+ years: olives, almonds, walnuts, chestnuts, pistachios, cork oak/holm oak, truffles, agroforestry, rural tourism accommodation if legal.

Best overall combinations:

Olive + herbs + bees + agro-tourism.
Chestnut/walnut + mushrooms + pigs/sheep + farm-stay.
Cork/holm oak montado + sheep/goats/pigs + honey + nature tourism.
Protected horticulture + seedlings + direct sales + compost.
Horse trekking + farm accommodation + local food experiences.
Aromatic plants + essential oils + workshops + online sales.
